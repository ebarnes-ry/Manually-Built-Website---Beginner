On Rothko at the Nat. Gallery

"The romantics were prompted to seek exotic subjects and to travel to far off places. They failed to realize that, though the transcendental must involve the strange and unfamiliar, not everything strange or unfamiliar is transcendental."

-Mark Rothko

Mark Rothko is one of the most celebrated painters of the 20th century. His works, characterized by large fields of color and subtle variations in hue, have left an indelible mark on the art world. The Rothko exhibit at the National Gallery in Washington DC offers a rare opportunity to see some of his most iconic pieces up close. Rothko was born in Latvia in 1903 and immigrated to the United States with his family when he was 10 years old. He studied painting at Yale University and later moved to New York City, where he became associated with the Abstract Expressionist movement. His works were often inspired by mystical ideas and sought to create a sense of transcendence through color and form.

The Rothko exhibit at the National Gallery features over 50 works spanning the artist's career. Visitors are greeted by a stunning mural of four enormous paintings that dominate the entrance to the exhibit. The paintings, which were originally created for a chapel in Houston, Texas, are a testament to the power of Rothko's work. They fill the space with an intense, pulsing energy that can be felt throughout the gallery. As visitors move through the exhibit, they are confronted by a series of Rothko's classic "multiform" paintings. These works are characterized by large areas of color that seem to shift and meld together as the viewer moves closer or farther away. Some of the paintings are strikingly simple—a single rectangle of red or blue—while others are more complex, with layers of color that seem to vibrate against one another.

One of the most fascinating aspects of the Rothko exhibit is the way in which the paintings are arranged. Rather than being arranged chronologically, the works are grouped thematically, allowing visitors to see how Rothko's ideas evolved over time. For example, one section of the exhibit focuses on Rothko's use of black, while another explores his interest in the natural world. Perhaps the most striking part of the exhibit is the final room, which features several canvases from Rothko's "Seagram Murals" series. These works, which were created for the Four Seasons restaurant in New York City, are among Rothko's most famous. They are characterized by large, rectangular fields of color that seem to glow from within. Visitors are encouraged to sit on benches facing the canvases, allowing them to be fully enveloped by Rothko's hypnotic colors.

In conclusion, the Rothko exhibit at the National Gallery in Washington DC is a must-see for anyone interested in modern art. The exhibit provides a rare opportunity to see some of Rothko's most iconic works up close, and the thematic arrangement of the paintings allows visitors to gain a deeper understanding of the artist's ideas and techniques. The exhibit is a testament to Rothko's enduring influence on the art world and his ability to create works that continue to captivate and inspire viewers more than half a century after they were created.
